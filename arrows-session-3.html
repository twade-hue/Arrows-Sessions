1

Juggling Warmup

Ball Mastery · Touch Sensitivity · Activation
10 MIN
Format: Individual → Pairs → Groups of 5
Balls: 1 per player
Space: Spread across half field

Carry the juggling warmup from Session 2 — players know the format. Three stages: individual (personal best), pairs (volley back-and-forth without touching the ground), then groups of 5 keeping the ball alive. Today's addition: in the group stage, each player must call a teammate's name before playing to them. Keeps communication sharp from the first minute.

Coaching Points
  • Locked ankle, relaxed leg — let the ball die on contact
  • Head up in the group stage — know your next option before you play
  • Call the name before the ball leaves your foot
Progression
  • Groups of 5: one player is "it" — if the ball hits the ground on their touch, they go in the middle and must win a touch before rejoining
  • Record group best — challenge it in Session 4

Rondo Bridge — 5v2 / 6v2

Press Resistance · Body Shape · Cognitive Prep
15 MIN
Groups: 2 groups of 7 (+ 6 waiting)
Format: 5v2 → 6v2
Space: 12×12 yd grids
Touch: 2 touch max

A bridge rondo before the main exercise. Players are split into the three-team structure they'll use in Phase 3 — teams of 5, 5, and 5 rotating through two grids (one team rests). This is the last "simple" rondo they'll do before the transfer game demands they press and transition simultaneously. Use it to dial in body shape and scanning habits.

Before moving to the transfer game, players need to understand the three-team structure. Use the last 2 minutes of this phase to explain Phase 3 setup — walk the teams to their zones, show the middle zone, and demonstrate one transfer sequence before the clock starts.

3

Bi-Rondal 4v4+4 — Transfer Game

All Four Phases · Counter Press · Zone Transfer
25 MIN
White — In Possession (Zone A)
Orange — In Possession (Zone B)
Blue — Pressing Team
Rondo 3 · Bi-Rondal 4v4+4 Transfer Game
2 zones of 10×15 m · 2×15 m middle zone · 20 players = 4 per team × 5 teams rotated in groups
Zones: 2 × 10×15 m end zones + 2×15 m middle
Teams: 3 groups of 5 (rotated as 4+1 sub)
Touch: Free → 2 touch in second half
Duration: 3 × 7 min rounds
Rotation: Blue presses for one full round then rotates

Structure: Three teams of 5 (run as 4+1 rotating sub). White keeps possession in Zone A. Orange keeps possession in Zone B. Blue presses — two players in Zone A creating a 4v2, two in the middle zone blocking the transfer lane.

The Transfer: White must combine in Zone A before switching the ball through or over the middle zone to Orange in Zone B. When the transfer is played, White's 4 move into the middle zone. Blue's 2 from Zone A sprint to intercept in the middle or press Orange in Zone B. Orange must receive and keep.

Turnover Rule: If Blue wins possession anywhere — in either zone or the middle — the team that lost possession becomes the new pressing team. Blue joins the team in possession. This is the counter-press trigger. Reward: Blue earns a point for each turnover; possession teams earn a point for every successful transfer.

Game Phase Which Team What They're Solving
Organised Attack White / Orange Build possession in zone, find the transfer moment, receive and keep in new zone
Transition to Attack Blue (on winning ball) Win possession → immediately join possession team → flip to attacking mindset in 1 second
Organised Defence Blue 4v2 press in zone, 2 middle players block transfer lane, work as coordinated unit
Transition to Defence White / Orange (on losing ball) Losing team immediately becomes new pressing team — reorganise and press within 3 seconds
TOVO

This is Todd Beane's "training game" — not a drill. All four phases of the game exist simultaneously in one exercise. The transfer moment is the session's core metaphor for field-switching; the counter-press trigger teaches players that the 3 seconds after losing possession are the most dangerous in football. Cruyff: "Football is a game of transitions."

Coaching Points — Possession Teams
  • Create triangles within the zone — don't let the 4v2 become 2v2 in one corner
  • Transfer decision: play it when the middle is open, not under desperation
  • Receiving team: one player steps to meet the transfer, others spread immediately
  • Use the 1st touch to set up the next pass — never receive and stand
  • Talk through the middle zone: call when the transfer is on
Coaching Points — Blue Press
  • 2 in zone: one hunts, one covers — never both chase the ball
  • Middle 2: split and close the transfer lane — don't sit central together
  • On turnover: sprint to join possession — transition is instant, not after a breath
  • Counter-press window: 3 seconds. After that, reorganise and defend the shape
Progressions
  • Reduce to 2-touch in both zones
  • Middle zone: possession team may not pass through — must dribble or loft
  • 2-point transfer: if receiving team strings 3 passes within 5 seconds of receiving, it counts double
20-Player Management
  • 3 teams of 5: each team plays 4 on the field, 1 rolls on as sub every 90 sec
  • Round rotation: after 7 min, the pressing team exits to rest, the resting team enters as the new press
  • Keep score across all rounds — team with most points at end wins
4

Phase of Play — Build & Transfer

Shape · Field-Switch · Transition Trigger
15 MIN
Format: 8v8 on ¾ field
Field: ~65×55 yd
Goals: Full-size, GKs included
Constraint: Must play through both wide channels before shooting
Subs: 4 players rotate in every 5 min

The bi-rondal transfer theme moves onto the full pitch. Two teams of 8 (+ GK). The wide channels represent Zone A and Zone B from the rondo — teams must use both touchline channels before shooting. This forces the same field-switch habit practiced in Phase 3 into a goal-to-goal context. Counter-press rule carries over: if a team loses possession in their own half, they must win it back before the other team crosses halfway.

Diagram · 8v8 Phase of Play with Wide Channel Rule
Coaching Points
  • Wingers hold width — same as Zone A/B in the rondo, just on a bigger field
  • The transfer moment in the rondo = switching play to the far wide player here
  • On loss: 3-second counter-press. Same trigger, same response as Phase 3
  • GK starts sequences — distribute wide under pressure, don't play into a press
Freeze Prompts (max 2)
  • "Where is the free zone right now — left or right?"
  • "How many seconds since we lost it — did we press?"
  • Point out the connection to the rondo — "This is the transfer. What did we do in the exercise?"
5

9v9 Free Game

Application · Compete · Express
20 MIN
Format: 9v9 inc. GKs
Field: Full or ¾ field
Constraint: None — free play
Duration: 2 × 8 min + 4 min end
Subs: Roll-on, all 20 active

No constraint. Let them play. After two phases of structured work, this is where the habits show up naturally — or don't. Keep stoppages to one per half maximum. Watch for the bi-rondal habits: field-switching, counter-press urgency, and supporting the ball carrier with two options. At the end, bring the group together and ask: "Where did you see the transfer game show up today?"

TOVO

The free game is how TOVO closes. The constraint trained the behavior; the free game reveals whether it's internalized. Don't over-coach. The two-question debrief at the end is more valuable than ten freezes during the game.

Coach's Note — Session 3 Intent

This session is the first time your players will feel all four phases of the game demanding a simultaneous response. The bi-rondal is cognitively hard — expect the first 5 minutes to be messy. Walk it before you run it. The middle zone is where most breakdowns happen: Blue's two middle players must be coached on their split positions before the round starts. The session's single connective thread is the transfer — from the rondo's zone switch to the phase of play's wide channel to the free game's natural field-switch moments. Close with the group question and let them identify the thread themselves.
