WARMUP — JUGGLING PROGRESSION

Ball Mastery · Touch Sensitivity · Body Activation
10 MIN
Format: Individual, then pairs
Space: Half field — spread out
Balls: 1 per player
Duration: 3 min individual · 4 min pairs · 3 min challenge

Every player starts with their own ball. Begin with free juggling to get touches — no pressure, just feel the ball. Coaches use this time to set up the rondo grid for Phase 2. Progress through three stages as a group on the coach's signal.

3-Stage Progression
  • Stage 1 (3 min) — Individual free juggling. Feet, thighs, chest. Count personal best.
  • Stage 2 (4 min) — Pairs juggling: Player A juggles 3 times, volleys to Player B. B controls and returns. Keep it alive — no ball on the ground.
  • Stage 3 (3 min) — Groups of 4: juggle and pass without the ball touching the ground. Count consecutive touches. Group record attempt.
Coaching Points
  • Relaxed ankle — locked ankles kill touch quality
  • Use the laces for height, instep for control
  • Head up in pairs — read your partner's body before they play it
  • Thigh juggles: let the ball die on contact, don't poke it
  • Keep score — competition raises intensity even in warmup
2

8V2 — PLAY WHAT YOU SEE

Scanning · 1st-Touch Decision · Cognitive Speed
15 MIN
Rondo 08 of 24 · Arrow Rondo Library 8V2 PLAY WHAT YOU SEE
10×10 m · 2 groups of 10
Groups: 2 × 10 players
Format: 6 perimeter + 2 mid vs. 2 def
Space: 10×10 m
Touch: 1st touch only
Rule: Play your 1st option — always

Six players on the perimeter, two teammates inside, two defenders inside. All eight keeping players must play their first-touch option immediately — no holding, no reassessing. Players scan before the ball arrives and commit to what they see. If a defender wins or forces an error, the player who lost it swaps in.

The two central players (#10, #8) are the key to this rondo — they act as midfielders, connecting perimeter passes and combining through the press.

↑ S1 LINK

Session 1 used a free-touch 4v1 arrival rondo. This is the direct upgrade — more players, two defenders, and a mandatory 1st-touch rule. Players already understand the format; today's rule pushes them to scan before the ball arrives, not after.

Coaching Points
  • Scan every time the ball leaves your feet — check both defenders
  • Body shape open: half-turn so you see pressure and space simultaneously
  • Central players: offer at an angle, never flat — give the passer a triangle
  • Perimeter players: if pressed, use the opposite side to switch the point immediately
  • Use it: "What did you see before you received?"
Progressions
  • Add a third defender — now 8v3, raise the pressure
  • Any pass that goes through a central player = 1 point. First to 5 wins
  • Perimeter players may enter — fully fluid, no fixed positions
  • Remove the 1-touch rule: 2-touch max, but reward 1-touch with a point
3

TECHNICAL — HEADING

Defensive Clearance · Attacking Flick · Aerial Duel
15 MIN
Groups: Pairs, then groups of 4
Space: 5–10 yd pairs · 15 yd for crossing
Balls: 1 per pair
Reps: 5 min per exercise

Three progressive exercises building from basic heading mechanics through to a live crossing-and-heading finish. Start all players in pairs — emphasize technique before adding movement or pressure. Every rep should be deliberate and coached.

Exercise A — Pairs: Toss, Head, Catch
Exercise B — Groups of 4: Head Across the Line
Exercise C — Live Cross & Head to Goal
Heading Coaching Points
  • Attack the ball — don't let it hit you; move toward it
  • Eyes open, mouth closed on contact — forehead, not hairline
  • Arch the back before heading; the snap forward generates power
  • Defensive header: head the top half of the ball to get height and distance
  • Attacking header: head down through the ball — aim at the corners
  • Run timing on the cross: arrive as the ball does, not before
Progressions
  • Exercise A: add a jump — B tosses higher, A must head at peak of jump
  • Exercise B: add a passive defender to create heading under pressure
  • Exercise C: add a second server alternating sides — attackers must read which cross is coming
  • Competition: 5 crosses, score each headed goal as a point
4

POSSESSION — 7V5 TARGET GAME

Field Switching · Target Play · Scanning in Congestion
20 MIN
Format: 7v5 in grid + 2 neutral targets
Grid: 35×30 yd
Touches: 2 touch
Scoring: Point for connecting target to target across field
Rounds: 3 × 5 min

One grid, 20 players: 7 keep, 5 press, plus one neutral target player stationed on each short end. The keeping team scores by playing through the grid and connecting from one target player to the other — meaning they must switch the field at some point. The pressing team scores by winning possession and stringing 5 passes. Targets are 1-touch only. Rotate the pressing team every 5 minutes.

Diagram · 7v5 Target Possession
↑ S1 LINK

Session 1 ran a 6v4 keep-away with no directional scoring. Today adds target players and a field-switch requirement — possession now has a purpose. Players must recognize when to play wide vs. when to retain centrally, replicating the decision problems in a real game.

Coaching Points
  • Can you see both target players from your position? Move until you can
  • Play into the target under low pressure, not after the press arrives
  • Target receives, plays back 1-touch — combination must continue immediately
  • Don't force the switch — build until the switch is on, then execute fast
  • Pressing team: cut off the passing lane to the far target first
Progressions
  • Target players can now step into the grid on receiving (true wingers)
  • 2-point bonus if switch is completed in under 3 passes from first target
  • Remove one keeper: 6v5 — sharper decisions under more pressure
5

9V9 — SHAPE GAME

Position Play · Transition Trigger · Compete
20 MIN
Format: 9v9 inc. GKs
Field: ~65×50 yd (¾ field)
Goals: Full-size both ends
Constraint: Must use both wide channels before shooting
Rotation: 2 subs per team roll on; 2 observe and analyze
Duration: 2 × 8 min

Teams of 9 (GK + 8 outfield). One attacking constraint: a team cannot shoot unless the ball has touched the wide channel on at least one side of the field in that possession sequence. This pushes width, field-switching, and all the principles covered in Phase 4. Roll-on subs keep all 20 active. Two players rotate out and are tasked with observing one specific player and giving feedback at the break.

Diagram · 9v9 with Wide Channel Constraint
↑ S1 LINK

Session 1 played a free 7v7. Today's game is 9v9 with a width constraint — same competitive energy, but the rule forces players to apply the field-switching and target-play learned in Phase 4. The two observer players create accountability and develop tactical awareness off the ball.

Coaching Points
  • Wide players: stay wide and available before the ball enters your channel
  • After using wide channel — attack the box quickly, don't reset
  • Lose it: immediate press toward the ball; don't wait to organize
  • GK: distribute to the wide player when under pressure — start the sequence
Observer Task
  • Watch one player only — how many times did they scan before receiving?
  • Did they use their 1st touch to set up a pass or buy time?
  • Report back at half-time break — player gives their own answer first

Coach's Note — Session 2 Intent

The juggling warmup is about feel and touch quality before any team demand — keep the energy light but competitive with personal bests and group records. The heading technical block is the most physical phase of the day, so sequence it before the possession work while legs are fresh. Three exercises build from mechanics to live finish, which is the right order: don't put players in a crossing scenario before they can head a simple toss cleanly. The rondo and possession game carry the session's core cognitive thread: see it before it arrives, play what you see. If you're stopping to coach, the question is almost always: "What did you see?" Let the SSG constraint do the tactical work for you and save your freezes for transition moments.
